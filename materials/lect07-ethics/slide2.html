Introduction to AI in GxP

  • Technological Advancement: AI provides new opportunities for the life sciences but introduces complexity and challenges in adoption at scale.
  • Purpose: The primary aim is to safeguard patient safety, product quality, and data integrity while achieving business benefits.
  • A Holistic Framework: The ISPE AI Guide serves as a bridge between GAMP® 5 concepts and the unique characteristics of AI.
  • Governance: Safe use is underpinned by organizational structures, policies, and literacy.

Life Cycle Phases

The AI-enabled computerized system life cycle follows four primary phases.

  1. Concept Phase: Identification of business needs, solution ideation, and conducting a Proof of Concept (PoC).
  2. Project Phase: Planning, model requirement specifications, iterative experimentation, and verification/testing.
  3. Operation Phase: Performance monitoring, managing model drift, and version control through change management.
  4. Retirement Phase: Data/model retention, migration, or destruction management.

Iterative Experimentation

At the core of the project phase is the iterative loop to derive the best-performing model.

  • Model Selection: Choosing model types and hyperparameters within the defined design space.
  • Data Engineering: Pre-processing data into a format suitable for the model.
  • Model Engineering: The training or fine-tuning process to establish the model.
  • Model Evaluation: Measuring performance against predefined KPIs to guide the next iteration.

Science-Based Quality Risk Management (QRM)

AI specific hazards must be addressed beyond traditional software risks.

  • Hazard Identification: Includes data quality shortcomings, inadequate model choice, and model drift during operation.
  • Functional Risk Assessment: AI functions are evaluated based on software category, model category, and AI maturity level.
  • Control Strategies: May include human verification (Human-in-the-Loop), guardrails for Generative AI, and Explainable AI (XAI) methods.

Fit for Purpose Data

A model is only as good as the data used to develop it. Data is “fit for purpose” when it meets four properties:

  1. Reliability: Real-world relationships are adequately captured.
  2. Relevance: Data is meaningful for the intended use.
  3. Representativeness: Data reflects the diversity of real-world situations.
  4. Abundance: The amount of data is commensurate with model complexity.

Trustworthy AI Principles

Deploying AI in life sciences requires adherence to human-centric values.

  • Human Autonomy: Ensuring humans can effectively interact with and supervise AI.
  • Transparency: Communicating benefits, risks, and limitations clearly to users.
  • Fairness: Reducing algorithmic bias to ensure equitable outcomes across subpopulations.
  • Safety & Security: Minimizing harm and protecting against AI-specific cybersecurity threats like adversarial attacks.

Conclusion & Governance

  • Accountability: Regulated companies remain accountable for compliance regardless of supplier involvement.
  • AI Literacy: Building foundational understanding across all roles (Process Owners, Data Scientists, Quality Units) is essential for successful adoption.
  • Continual Improvement: New data collected during operation should be used to refine and improve AI sub-systems over time.
